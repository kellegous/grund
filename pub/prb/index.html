On Google+, I asked the following question:
I'm going to run this contest where I call N of your friends and ask them to pick a number between 1 and 100. If any of your friends pick the same number, I give you $100. If none of them pick the same number, I give you nothing. Each friend will cost you $1. How many friends do you want me to call?

The problem got stuck in my head on that Sunday because I was trying to think of problems that would demonstrate the extent to which our intuition misleads us when a question involves expected value. It's not a great example, but it stuck in my head until I sat down and did the math.
This is actually just the class birthday problem with an expected value component tacked on. If you look at that Wikipedia page, you'll understand why the probability of winning looks like this. The reason this is a “classic” is because the probability grows much faster that intuition would suggest.
To determine the number of friends to call, we're looking to maximize expected value. That's just P(win) * $100 - cost (# of friends). Plotting the expected value, it is clear that there is a point where adding more friends will not do much to increase the chance of winning, but it will cost more. That sweet spot is at around 25 friends. That makes the cost, of course, is a mere $25 and the chance of winning is 95%.
